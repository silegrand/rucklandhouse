Behind the Name

A Name with
Gravesend at its Heart.

"Pocahontas died in Gravesend in 1617, on the eve of her return to Virginia. She never made it home. But Gravesend has never forgotten her."

Amonute — known to history as Pocahontas — was a Powhatan woman who became one of the most significant figures in the early colonial encounter between Native America and England. She travelled to London in 1616, where she was received at court. She died in Gravesend in March 1617, aged around 21, before her ship could return her to Virginia.

She is buried at St George's Church, just minutes from Ruckland House. The town has honoured her memory ever since — in the name of this room, in a statue on the riverside, and in the town's abiding sense of its own place in history.

Naming this room after her is not a gimmick. It's a quiet acknowledgement that Gravesend is a town with a remarkable story — and that the building you're in is part of it.

1617

Pocahontas died in Gravesend on 21 March 1617, aged approximately 21. She is buried at St George's Church, a few minutes' walk from Ruckland House. A bronze statue on Gravesend's riverside commemorates her life and her connection to the town. She remains one of the most remarkable figures associated with this corner of Kent.
