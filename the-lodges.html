"We are just a group of ordinary people
who like to have fun — and help out others."

That's how the Freemasons of East Kent describe themselves. No grand claims, no mystique. Just men from every walk of life — doctors, tradespeople, teachers, retired pilots, young professionals — who share a common belief: that you leave the world slightly better than you found it. The lodges at Ruckland House have been doing exactly that since 1905. This is their story.

What the lodges have done

Charity Is Not
a Side Project.

Across East Kent, Masonic lodges have quietly funded food banks, children's education, community health projects, and emergency support for families in crisis. Here is a small part of what that looks like in practice.

Children & Young People

Supporting Education Across Kent

The Cornwallis East Kent Freemasons' Charity has directed over £35,000 specifically to support the education of children and young people across the county — funding equipment, experiences, and opportunities that families in financial difficulty could not otherwise access.

£35,555 To children's education in East Kent
Health & Wellbeing

Care for People in Crisis

Over £300,000 has been granted to help cover the daily living costs of individuals and families experiencing genuine hardship — and a further £167,000 to support health, care and wellbeing. These are not abstract figures. They represent real people in Kent whose lives were materially changed.

£504,047 In grants to individuals & families
Community

Food Banks & Emergency Relief

When the Thanington Food Bank needed support, East Kent Freemasons were there with a donation. When East Kent Hospitals needed colouring books for patients — children, elderly, those in long-term care — 3,000 were delivered by hand. Small gestures, real impact.

3,000 Books delivered to East Kent Hospitals
Young People

Restoring a Sensory Garden for Scouts

In 2024, a lodge noticed a Scout group in Maidstone had a sensory garden that had fallen into disrepair — vital for young people with physical and mental needs. Freemasons fundraised, applied for grants, and on two weekends showed up with shovels and wheelbarrows. The garden was restored.

£6,000 Masonic Charitable Foundation grant awarded
Disability & Inclusion

Fishing Days for Special Needs Schools

The East Kent Masonic Fishing Charity takes students from special needs schools and centres — and people who have experienced trauma — to spend a day fishing in the countryside. The students look forward to it all year. No agenda. Just a good day out.

Annual Countryside experiences for students
Armed Forces

Supporting Those Who Served

East Kent Freemasons are proud signatories of the Armed Forces Covenant, working to ensure that veterans, serving personnel, and their families are treated with fairness and respect. It reflects a principle central to Freemasonry: that service is not just a value, it's a practice.

So what is it?

Freemasonry
in Plain English.

Freemasonry is one of the world's oldest membership organisations — founded on the idea that men of good character, whatever their background, profession or faith, can come together around shared values and do something useful with that connection.

It is not a religion. It has no political affiliation. It does not ask you to believe anything you don't already believe. What it does ask is that you commit to treating people with integrity, to giving something back, and to showing up — for the lodge, and for the community around it.

In England, Freemasonry has existed for over 300 years. The United Grand Lodge of England (UGLE) oversees hundreds of lodges across the country, each one a local community of members who meet regularly, observe centuries-old ceremonial traditions, and raise money for charity.

"Having no political or religious affiliations, we comprise members of all ages, races, religions, cultures and backgrounds — who make time to eat, drink and meet together, and form lifelong friendships." United Grand Lodge of England
I
Integrity

Acting honestly and with good conscience — in the lodge, in business, and in everyday life. It is the first thing a Freemason commits to, and the standard by which members hold each other.

II
Friendship

Lodge membership creates genuine bonds across the usual divides of profession, background and age. A retired headteacher and an apprentice plumber can share the same table, and often do. That's not incidental to Freemasonry — it's central to it.

III
Respect

For people of all faiths, none, and every background. Freemasonry has no doctrine beyond the expectation that members treat others with the same dignity they would want for themselves.

IV
Service

Giving back to the community — through charitable donations, volunteer work, and direct action. Not as an obligation, but as an expression of what membership stands for. In East Kent alone, £430,000 has been donated since 2014.

At Ruckland House

The Lodges That
Call This Place Home.

Several lodges have met at Ruckland House for generations. Each has its own history, its own character, and its own place in the life of Gravesend.

No. 483

Lodge of Sympathy

One of the older lodges meeting at Ruckland House, the Lodge of Sympathy carries a name that speaks to the founding spirit of Masonic charity — a concern for others that has been its guiding principle since the nineteenth century. A long and distinguished presence in Gravesend's Masonic life.

No. 6433

Libertas Secunda Lodge

Founded in 1947 by local river pilots — men who guided ships through the Thames estuary and knew Gravesend's waterfront as well as anyone alive. The name means "Second Freedom" — a freedom earned through service and brotherhood. Their story is inseparable from the town's history as a working river port.

No. 6454

Chantry Lodge

Named for the historic Chantry heritage of the area, this lodge continues the tradition of community engagement and charitable giving that has characterised the lodges at Ruckland House throughout their time here. A committed presence in the East Kent Masonic community.

No. 7269

Cobham Lodge

Taking its name from nearby Cobham — home to Cobham Hall and the Darnley family who were among the early owners of Ruckland House itself — Cobham Lodge connects the building's heritage to the wider history of North Kent. A lodge with a sense of place and a strong local identity.

Setting the record straight

What People Think.
What Is Actually True.

Freemasonry has accumulated more myths than almost any other organisation in history. Most of them are wrong. Here are the most common ones.

The myth

"It's a secret society with hidden agendas."

The reality

Freemasonry is a society with private traditions — not a secret organisation. Lodge locations, membership, and charitable activities are all publicly available. What happens in a meeting isn't broadcast, but it isn't hidden either.

The myth

"You have to be invited, and only the right sort of person gets in."

The reality

Any man of good character can enquire about joining. The UGLE actively welcomes enquiries from men of all backgrounds, ages, faiths and professions. You don't need to know a member — you can simply ask.

The myth

"It's a religious organisation."

The reality

Freemasonry has no creed, no doctrine, and no affiliation to any religion. It asks only that members believe in a Supreme Being — defined entirely according to their own faith or philosophy. Christians, Muslims, Jews, Sikhs and many others are members.

The myth

"Members help each other at the expense of outsiders."

The reality

Freemasonry explicitly prohibits members from using their membership for personal or professional advantage. The charitable giving — over £42 million nationally in a single year — goes overwhelmingly to people with no connection to Freemasonry at all.
