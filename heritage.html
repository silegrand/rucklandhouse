The Early Victorian Era

A Gentleman's Mansion
on Wrotham Road

Ruckland House takes its name from its original proprietor, Lawrence Ruck, a Gravesend grocer of considerable standing who built the mansion house on Wrotham Road in the early decades of the nineteenth century. The house was recorded in contemporary accounts as a "large mansion" — a descriptor that speaks to both its physical scale and the social ambitions of the merchant class that lined this stretch of road south of the town centre.

The timing was no accident. In the first half of the nineteenth century, Gravesend was at the height of its prosperity. With the coming of the steamboats and the construction of the town's piers, thousands of day-trippers poured in from London. Business boomed, pleasure grounds opened, and the most fashionable of these — Rosherville Gardens — rivalled the Vauxhall Gardens in London itself. Wrotham Road, turnpiked in 1825 and the main artery south from the riverside, was precisely where a successful merchant of the era would choose to build.

Image: Gravesend c.1830s

The Ruck family's commercial prosperity underwrote a substantial private estate. Its grounds extended well beyond the house itself, encompassing open land to the south that would soon find a singular purpose — and in doing so would shape the sporting history of the entire county of Kent.

The house remained in private family occupation for several decades before passing through the hands of successive owners, including the Darnley family of Cobham Hall and the Billings family, each of whom maintained the property and its grounds.

The grounds of Ruckland House enter the historical record most vividly through their connection to cricket. The first recorded match on the site took place in 1840, when a Gravesend team played Penenden Heath — a fixture of local significance at a time when cricket was becoming central to Kent's civic identity.

"The cricket ground was laid out in the grounds of Ruckland House, described as a 'large mansion', and used as the private cricket ground for the house."

Howard Milton — Bat and Ball Gravesend: A First-class Cricket History (1999)

Around 1845, Thomas Miles Adams (1810–1894) — a lifelong Gravesend resident and one of Kent's most distinguished cricketers — laid a new wicket in the mansion grounds, levelling and turfing the site using turf brought from Cobham Park. Adams had played for Kent since 1836, also representing the Marylebone Cricket Club (MCC) and various All England Elevens. He made over 150 appearances in important matches and later umpired a further 20 between 1852 and 1865.

Under Adams's stewardship, possibly with fellow Gravesend cricketer William Smith, the private ground was transformed into a venue of county and eventually national standing. In 1849 — just four years after Adams laid the wicket — Kent County Cricket Club played their first first-class match on the site, against an All-England Eleven. A ground born from the leisure of a single merchant family had become a stage for the highest level of English cricket.

History on the Ground

Moments That
Made History

The ground that Lawrence Ruck's estate made possible went on to host two of the most remarkable moments in the history of English cricket — moments connected not just to the ground itself, but to the town of Gravesend.

In May 1868, the first Australian cricket team ever to tour England sailed into Gravesend aboard the ship Paramatta after a three-month voyage from Sydney. The thirteen players were First Nations men — Jardwadjali, Gunditjmara and Wotjobaluk peoples from the western district of Victoria — captained by the English cricketer Charles Lawrence. They were taken to lunch at the Bat and Ball Inn beside the ground before playing one of their earliest matches on the very turf that Lawrence Ruck's estate had made possible.

It was a tour of profound significance. The players went on to contest 47 matches across England that summer, winning 14 and drawing 19. It would be another ten years before a second Australian team followed them. Gravesend — and the ground born from a merchant's private estate — was where it all began.

Image: Aboriginal XI, 1868 Tour of England

"He received three hearty cheers as he made his way along the street to his hotel — a triumphal procession through the town."

Bristol Times and Mirror, on W. G. Grace at Gravesend, 1895

In 1895, W. G. Grace — already a legend at 46 — scored 257 for Gloucestershire against Kent on the ground, the highest individual innings ever recorded there. He was on the field for every single ball of the match: scoring 257, remaining unbeaten on 73 in the second innings, and bowling 43 overs in Kent's first innings of 470. When he finally left the field, the crowd had waited for him to change. He emerged from the pavilion to three hearty cheers and made his way along the street to his hotel in what reporters called a triumphal procession through the town. The Bristol Times and Mirror considered it a record no other player had equalled. He was approaching his 47th birthday.

1905
A New Chapter

The Masons
& a New Purpose

In 1905, Ruckland House entered a new phase of its long life. The property on Wrotham Road was purchased for the sum of £2,100 to serve as a Masonic Temple — a transaction recorded in the civic annals of Gravesend that year alongside the purchase of the town cemetery and the extension of the electricity supply to Northfleet.

The Gravesend Masonic Hall Company Limited was formally incorporated on 16 June 1905, with its registered address at 25 Wrotham Road — the same address the building holds to this day. The Company remains active, a continuous institutional presence spanning over one hundred and twenty years.

"Through the years it has been transformed and extended to allow its progression from a large family home to the business it is today. With the retention of many of its original features, it makes it a truly quirky and comfortable space."

Ruckland House — official description

Through the twentieth century, Ruckland House was adapted to meet the needs of its new occupants while retaining much of its Victorian character. Extensions were added, rooms reconfigured, and the building evolved from a private family residence into a functioning Masonic centre serving the lodges of East Kent.

Several lodges have met regularly at the Hall, among them the Lodge of Sympathy No. 483, Chantry Lodge No. 6454, Cobham Lodge No. 7269, and Libertas Secunda Lodge No. 6433 — the last founded in 1947 by local river pilots, reflecting Gravesend's deep and enduring ties to the Thames. The Masonic stewardship brought with it a philosophy of community service, charitable giving, and civic involvement that has been consistent with the building's wider history.

Ruckland House today operates as both the Gravesend Masonic Centre and a well-regarded events venue serving the wider community. The building at 25 Wrotham Road hosts weddings, celebrations, meetings, and family occasions — a continuation of its role as a gathering place at the heart of Gravesend life.

The original structure survives as the core of the building, with many of its Victorian features intact. The rooms bear witness to nearly two centuries of continuous occupation: as a merchant's family seat, as the cradle of county cricket, and as a centre of fraternal life in East Kent.

Few buildings in Gravesend carry so layered a heritage within a single address — a quiet mansion on a busy road that once shaped the landscape of English cricket, and has served the community without interruption for nearly two hundred years.
